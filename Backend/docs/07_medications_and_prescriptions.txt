SECTION: Understanding Prescriptions
A prescription is a written or electronic instruction from a licensed healthcare professional authorising a patient to receive a specific medication. Prescriptions typically include the patient's name and date of birth, the name of the medication (generic or brand name), the dosage and strength, instructions for use (how much to take and how often), the quantity to dispense, the number of refills permitted, and the prescribing doctor's signature. Always read the label and accompanying information leaflet before taking any medication. If you have any questions about your prescription, ask your pharmacist or doctor for clarification before starting the medication.

SECTION: Generic vs Brand Name Medications
Most medications have both a generic name and one or more brand names. The generic name is the official medical name for the active ingredient. The brand name is given by the pharmaceutical company that first developed the drug. Generic medications contain the same active ingredient in the same dose and form as the brand name drug and are required to meet the same safety and efficacy standards. Generic medications are typically significantly cheaper than brand name equivalents. Switching to a generic version of your medication is usually safe, but always inform your doctor or pharmacist if you have concerns or notice any difference in effect.

SECTION: Common Over-the-Counter Medications
Over-the-counter (OTC) medications can be purchased without a prescription. Common examples include paracetamol (acetaminophen) for pain and fever relief, ibuprofen for pain, inflammation, and fever, antihistamines such as loratadine or cetirizine for allergies, antacids such as omeprazole or calcium carbonate for heartburn and indigestion, and loperamide for diarrhoea. Always follow dosage instructions and do not exceed the recommended dose. OTC medications can interact with prescription drugs and may be unsuitable for people with certain health conditions. Consult a pharmacist or doctor if you are unsure whether an OTC medication is safe for you.

SECTION: Paracetamol (Acetaminophen) – Safe Use
Paracetamol is one of the most widely used pain relievers and fever reducers. The standard adult dose is 500mg to 1000mg every 4 to 6 hours, with a maximum of 4000mg (4g) in 24 hours. Paracetamol is generally safe when taken as directed but can cause serious liver damage if taken in excessive doses. Alcohol increases the risk of liver damage when taking paracetamol. Many combination medications such as cold and flu remedies also contain paracetamol, so it is important to check all product labels to avoid accidental overdose. Paracetamol overdose, even if not intentional, is a medical emergency requiring immediate hospital treatment.

SECTION: Ibuprofen and NSAIDs – Safe Use
Ibuprofen belongs to the class of drugs known as non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs). It relieves pain, reduces inflammation, and lowers fever. The standard adult dose is 200mg to 400mg every 4 to 6 hours, with a maximum of 1200mg per day for OTC use. NSAIDs should always be taken with food to reduce the risk of stomach irritation. They are not recommended for people with stomach ulcers, kidney disease, heart failure, or a history of cardiovascular disease. NSAIDs should be avoided during the third trimester of pregnancy. Other NSAIDs include naproxen, aspirin, diclofenac, and celecoxib. Long-term use should be supervised by a doctor.

SECTION: Antibiotics – What You Need to Know
Antibiotics are medications used to treat infections caused by bacteria. They are not effective against viruses and should not be used to treat viral infections such as the common cold or flu. Common classes of antibiotics include penicillins (such as amoxicillin), cephalosporins, macrolides (such as azithromycin), and fluoroquinolones (such as ciprofloxacin). It is essential to complete the full prescribed course of antibiotics, even if you feel better before finishing, to prevent antibiotic resistance and ensure the infection is fully cleared. Never take antibiotics prescribed for someone else. Common side effects include nausea, diarrhoea, and in some cases, allergic reactions. Antibiotic resistance is a growing global health threat caused by overuse and misuse of antibiotics.

SECTION: Medication Adherence and Missed Doses
Medication adherence refers to taking medications as prescribed, at the correct dose, at the correct times, and for the full prescribed duration. Non-adherence is a major cause of treatment failure and disease progression. Strategies to improve adherence include setting reminders on your phone, using a pill organiser, linking medication taking to a daily routine such as mealtimes, and keeping a medication diary. If you miss a dose, check the information leaflet or consult your pharmacist for guidance on what to do. Do not double up on doses unless specifically instructed to do so. If you regularly forget doses, speak to your doctor about whether a simpler dosing schedule is possible.

SECTION: Drug Interactions
A drug interaction occurs when one medication affects the way another medication works. Interactions can reduce the effectiveness of a drug, increase the risk of side effects, or cause unexpected effects. Common clinically significant interactions include warfarin with many antibiotics, NSAIDs, and certain herbal supplements such as St John's Wort; statins with certain antibiotics and antifungals; oral contraceptives with some antibiotics and anticonvulsants; and metformin with contrast dye used in certain medical imaging procedures. Always tell your doctor and pharmacist about all medications you are taking, including prescription drugs, OTC medications, vitamins, and herbal supplements. Never start or stop a medication without consulting your healthcare provider.

SECTION: Side Effects of Medications
Side effects are unwanted effects that occur in addition to the intended therapeutic effect of a medication. They range from mild and temporary to severe and requiring medical attention. Common mild side effects include nausea, headache, drowsiness, dry mouth, and constipation. Serious side effects that require immediate medical attention include severe allergic reactions (anaphylaxis), chest pain, difficulty breathing, severe skin reactions, and signs of liver or kidney problems such as yellowing of the skin, dark urine, or unusual swelling. All medications can potentially cause side effects. The information leaflet included with every medication lists known side effects. If you experience an unexpected or concerning side effect, contact your doctor or pharmacist.

SECTION: Storing Medications Safely
Proper storage of medications ensures they remain safe and effective. Most medications should be stored at room temperature, away from heat, light, and moisture. Avoid storing medications in the bathroom, as humidity can degrade them. Some medications, such as certain insulin products and eye drops, require refrigeration. Keep all medications in their original containers with labels intact. Store medications out of reach of children and pets. Check expiry dates regularly and safely dispose of expired or unused medications. Do not flush medications down the toilet unless the label specifically instructs you to. Many pharmacies offer medication disposal services.

SECTION: Medications in Pregnancy and Breastfeeding
Many medications are not safe during pregnancy or breastfeeding. During pregnancy, medications can cross the placenta and affect the developing foetus. Some are known to cause birth defects (teratogenic), while others may affect foetal growth or development. Always inform your doctor if you are pregnant, planning to become pregnant, or breastfeeding before starting any new medication. Paracetamol is generally considered the safest pain reliever during pregnancy. NSAIDs should be avoided, particularly in the first and third trimesters. Some prescription medications for chronic conditions such as epilepsy, diabetes, and mental health disorders may need to be continued during pregnancy under careful medical supervision with risk-benefit assessment.

SECTION: Long-Term Medication Management
Many patients with chronic conditions take medications on a long-term or permanent basis. Examples include antihypertensives for high blood pressure, statins for high cholesterol, metformin for type 2 diabetes, levothyroxine for hypothyroidism, and antidepressants for depression. Long-term medication use requires regular monitoring including blood tests, blood pressure checks, and review of ongoing need and dosage. Medication reviews should be conducted at least annually by your GP or specialist. Never stop a long-term medication abruptly without medical advice, as this can lead to withdrawal effects or a rebound of the underlying condition. If you are experiencing side effects or concerns about a long-term medication, discuss alternatives or dose adjustments with your doctor.
