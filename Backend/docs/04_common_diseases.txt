SECTION: Common Cold
The common cold is a viral infection of the upper respiratory tract, primarily caused by rhinoviruses. It is one of the most frequent illnesses in humans. Symptoms include runny or blocked nose, sore throat, sneezing, cough, mild headache, and low-grade fever. Symptoms typically appear 1 to 3 days after exposure and last 7 to 10 days. There is no cure for the common cold. Treatment is supportive: rest, staying hydrated, and over-the-counter medications to relieve symptoms. Antibiotics are not effective against viral infections. The cold spreads through respiratory droplets and contact with contaminated surfaces. Frequent handwashing and avoiding close contact with infected individuals helps prevent transmission.

SECTION: Influenza (Flu)
Influenza is a contagious respiratory illness caused by influenza viruses. It is more severe than the common cold and can lead to serious complications. Symptoms include sudden onset of fever, chills, muscle aches, headache, fatigue, dry cough, sore throat, and runny nose. Complications can include pneumonia, ear infections, sinus infections, and worsening of chronic conditions. High-risk groups include the elderly, young children, pregnant women, and people with chronic health conditions. Annual flu vaccination is the most effective prevention. Antiviral medications such as oseltamivir (Tamiflu) can reduce severity if taken within 48 hours of symptom onset.

SECTION: Type 2 Diabetes
Type 2 diabetes is a chronic condition in which the body does not produce enough insulin or does not use insulin effectively, leading to elevated blood sugar levels. It is the most common form of diabetes, accounting for around 90% of all diabetes cases. Risk factors include obesity, physical inactivity, family history, age over 45, and high blood pressure. Symptoms include frequent urination, increased thirst, unexplained weight loss, fatigue, blurred vision, and slow-healing wounds. Long-term complications affect the heart, kidneys, eyes, and nerves. Management includes lifestyle changes (diet and exercise), oral medications such as metformin, and in some cases insulin therapy.

SECTION: Hypertension (High Blood Pressure)
Hypertension is a condition where the force of blood against artery walls is consistently too high. It is defined as a blood pressure reading of 140/90 mmHg or higher. It affects approximately 1 in 4 adults globally and is a leading risk factor for heart attack, stroke, and kidney disease. Most people with hypertension have no symptoms, which is why regular blood pressure checks are important. Risk factors include age, obesity, physical inactivity, high salt intake, smoking, alcohol, and family history. Treatment includes lifestyle modifications and antihypertensive medications such as ACE inhibitors, calcium channel blockers, or beta-blockers.

SECTION: Asthma
Asthma is a chronic inflammatory condition of the airways that causes them to narrow and swell, producing excess mucus. This makes breathing difficult and triggers coughing, wheezing, and shortness of breath. Asthma can be triggered by allergens (pollen, dust mites, pet dander), exercise, cold air, respiratory infections, and air pollution. It affects people of all ages but often begins in childhood. Asthma is managed with reliever inhalers (such as salbutamol) for acute symptoms and preventer inhalers (containing corticosteroids) for long-term control. Severe asthma attacks require emergency medical treatment. Asthma cannot be cured but can be well controlled.

SECTION: Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease (GERD)
GERD is a digestive condition where stomach acid frequently flows back into the oesophagus, irritating its lining. Common symptoms include heartburn (a burning sensation in the chest), acid regurgitation, difficulty swallowing, chronic cough, and a sour taste in the mouth. Symptoms are often worse after eating, when lying down, or bending over. Risk factors include obesity, pregnancy, smoking, and certain foods such as fatty meals, caffeine, and alcohol. GERD is treated with lifestyle changes, antacids, H2 receptor blockers, and proton pump inhibitors (PPIs). Untreated GERD can lead to oesophagitis, Barrett's oesophagus, and oesophageal cancer.

SECTION: Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)
A urinary tract infection is an infection in any part of the urinary system, including the kidneys, bladder, ureters, and urethra. Most UTIs involve the lower urinary tract (bladder and urethra). Common symptoms include a burning sensation when urinating, frequent urge to urinate, passing small amounts of urine, cloudy or strong-smelling urine, pelvic pain, and blood in the urine. UTIs are far more common in women due to the shorter urethra. They are most commonly caused by the bacterium Escherichia coli (E. coli). Treatment involves a course of antibiotics. Drinking plenty of water helps flush bacteria from the urinary tract.

SECTION: Depression
Depression is a common and serious mental health condition characterised by persistent feelings of sadness, hopelessness, and loss of interest in activities once enjoyed. It affects how a person feels, thinks, and handles daily activities. Symptoms include low mood, loss of energy, sleep disturbances, changes in appetite, difficulty concentrating, feelings of worthlessness, and in severe cases, thoughts of self-harm or suicide. Depression is caused by a combination of genetic, biological, environmental, and psychological factors. Treatment includes psychotherapy (such as cognitive behavioural therapy), antidepressant medications, lifestyle changes, and in severe cases, specialist psychiatric care.

SECTION: Anxiety Disorders
Anxiety disorders are a group of mental health conditions characterised by excessive, persistent fear or worry that interferes with daily life. Types include generalised anxiety disorder (GAD), panic disorder, social anxiety disorder, and specific phobias. Symptoms include restlessness, rapid heartbeat, sweating, trembling, difficulty breathing, sleep problems, and difficulty concentrating. Anxiety disorders are among the most common mental health conditions globally. Treatment includes cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT), medication (such as SSRIs or benzodiazepines), relaxation techniques, and lifestyle modifications including exercise and reducing caffeine intake.

SECTION: Osteoarthritis
Osteoarthritis is the most common form of arthritis and occurs when the protective cartilage that cushions the ends of bones wears down over time. It most commonly affects the joints in the hands, knees, hips, and spine. Symptoms include joint pain, stiffness (especially in the morning or after rest), swelling, reduced range of motion, and a grating sensation during movement. Risk factors include age, obesity, previous joint injury, and repetitive joint stress. There is no cure for osteoarthritis, but treatment aims to reduce pain and improve function through weight management, physiotherapy, pain relief medications, and in advanced cases, joint replacement surgery.

SECTION: Anaemia
Anaemia is a condition in which the body lacks enough healthy red blood cells to carry adequate oxygen to the body's tissues. The most common type is iron-deficiency anaemia, often caused by poor diet, blood loss, or poor iron absorption. Other types include vitamin B12 deficiency anaemia, folate deficiency, and anaemia of chronic disease. Symptoms include fatigue, weakness, pale skin, shortness of breath, dizziness, cold hands and feet, and brittle nails. Treatment depends on the underlying cause and may include dietary changes, iron or vitamin supplements, or treatment of an underlying condition. Severe anaemia may require blood transfusions.
