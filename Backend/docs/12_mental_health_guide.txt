SECTION: Understanding Mental Health
Mental health refers to our emotional, psychological, and social wellbeing. It affects how we think, feel, and behave, and influences how we handle stress, relate to others, and make decisions. Good mental health is not the absence of mental illness — it is the ability to cope with the normal stresses of life, work productively, and contribute to your community. Mental health conditions are common: approximately 1 in 4 people in the UK will experience a mental health problem each year. Mental health conditions are caused by a complex interaction of genetic, biological, psychological, and environmental factors. They are not a sign of personal weakness or a character flaw, and they are treatable.

SECTION: Depression – Causes, Symptoms, and Treatment
Depression is a mood disorder characterised by persistent feelings of sadness, emptiness, or hopelessness that last for at least 2 weeks and interfere with daily functioning. It is one of the most common mental health conditions globally. Symptoms include persistent low mood, loss of interest or pleasure in activities previously enjoyed (anhedonia), changes in appetite and weight, sleep disturbances (insomnia or oversleeping), fatigue and low energy, difficulty concentrating or making decisions, feelings of worthlessness or excessive guilt, and in severe cases, recurrent thoughts of death or suicide. Depression is not simply feeling sad — it is a serious medical condition. Treatment includes psychological therapies such as cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT), antidepressant medications particularly SSRIs (selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors), lifestyle changes, and social support.

SECTION: Anxiety Disorders – Types and Management
Anxiety is a normal human response to perceived threat or stress. However, when anxiety becomes persistent, excessive, and out of proportion to the actual situation, it may indicate an anxiety disorder. Generalised anxiety disorder (GAD) involves persistent, uncontrollable worry about a wide range of everyday things. Panic disorder involves recurrent unexpected panic attacks — sudden intense surges of fear with physical symptoms including racing heart, sweating, trembling, and a sense of impending doom. Social anxiety disorder involves intense fear of social situations and scrutiny by others. Specific phobias are intense fears of particular objects or situations. Treatment for anxiety disorders includes CBT (the most evidence-based psychological treatment), exposure therapy, medication (SSRIs or SNRIs as first-line, benzodiazepines only short-term), and mindfulness-based approaches.

SECTION: Bipolar Disorder
Bipolar disorder is a serious mental health condition characterised by extreme mood swings that include emotional highs (mania or hypomania) and lows (depression). During manic episodes, a person may feel euphoric, have excess energy, sleep very little, engage in impulsive or reckless behaviour, and feel grandiose or invincible. Depressive episodes involve the classic symptoms of depression described above. Bipolar I disorder involves full manic episodes. Bipolar II disorder involves hypomanic episodes (less severe than full mania) and depressive episodes. Bipolar disorder is a lifelong condition but can be effectively managed with mood-stabilising medications such as lithium, valproate, or quetiapine, combined with psychological therapies and lifestyle management. Early recognition and treatment are important for reducing the frequency and severity of episodes.

SECTION: Schizophrenia and Psychosis
Psychosis is a condition in which a person loses touch with reality. It can involve hallucinations (perceiving things that are not there, commonly hearing voices), delusions (strongly held false beliefs that do not change with evidence), disorganised thinking and speech, and confused or disturbed behaviour. Schizophrenia is a severe and long-term mental health condition that includes psychosis along with negative symptoms such as social withdrawal, emotional flatness, and reduced motivation. It affects around 1 in 100 people. A first episode of psychosis is a medical emergency. Early intervention significantly improves outcomes. Treatment includes antipsychotic medications, psychological therapies, community support, and rehabilitation. Most people with schizophrenia can live fulfilling lives with appropriate treatment and support.

SECTION: Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD)
PTSD is a mental health condition triggered by experiencing or witnessing a traumatic event. Traumatic events can include assault, abuse, natural disasters, accidents, combat, and medical emergencies. Not everyone who experiences trauma develops PTSD. Symptoms include intrusive re-experiencing of the trauma (flashbacks, nightmares), avoidance of reminders of the trauma, negative changes in thoughts and mood, and hyperarousal (being easily startled, feeling tense, having difficulty sleeping). Symptoms must persist for more than a month to meet the PTSD diagnosis. Complex PTSD (C-PTSD) can develop after prolonged or repeated trauma, particularly in childhood. Effective treatments include trauma-focused CBT and EMDR (eye movement desensitisation and reprocessing). Medication may also be used alongside therapy.

SECTION: Eating Disorders
Eating disorders are serious mental health conditions characterised by disturbances in eating behaviour and a preoccupation with food, weight, and body image. Anorexia nervosa involves restriction of food intake leading to significantly low body weight, an intense fear of gaining weight, and distorted body image. Bulimia nervosa involves cycles of binge eating followed by compensatory behaviours such as self-induced vomiting, laxative use, or excessive exercise. Binge eating disorder involves recurrent episodes of eating large amounts of food rapidly, feeling out of control, and experiencing shame or guilt, without compensatory behaviours. Eating disorders have the highest mortality rate of any mental health condition. Early intervention is critical. Treatment is multi-disciplinary and includes nutritional rehabilitation, psychological therapy (particularly CBT-ED), medical monitoring, and family therapy for younger patients.

SECTION: Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD)
OCD is a chronic mental health condition characterised by obsessions (unwanted, intrusive, distressing thoughts, images, or urges) and compulsions (repetitive behaviours or mental acts performed to reduce the anxiety caused by obsessions). Common obsessions include fears of contamination, fears of causing harm, and preoccupations with symmetry or order. Common compulsions include excessive handwashing, checking, counting, arranging, and mental rituals. OCD is not about being overly neat or clean — it is a debilitating condition that can severely impact quality of life. OCD affects around 1 to 2% of people. Treatment includes CBT with exposure and response prevention (ERP), which is considered the most effective psychological treatment, and SSRIs at higher doses than used for depression.

SECTION: Sleep Disorders and Mental Health
Sleep and mental health have a bidirectional relationship — poor mental health disrupts sleep, and poor sleep worsens mental health. Common sleep disorders include insomnia (difficulty falling or staying asleep), sleep apnoea (breathing interruptions during sleep causing repeated awakenings), restless legs syndrome, and hypersomnia (excessive daytime sleepiness). Insomnia is closely associated with depression, anxiety, and bipolar disorder. Cognitive behavioural therapy for insomnia (CBT-I) is the most effective first-line treatment for chronic insomnia and is preferred over sleep medications. Sleep hygiene measures include maintaining a consistent sleep schedule, avoiding screens in the hour before bed, keeping the bedroom cool and dark, limiting caffeine after midday, and avoiding alcohol as a sleep aid as it impairs sleep quality.

SECTION: Seeking Mental Health Support
If you are experiencing mental health difficulties, there are several routes to support. Your GP is a good first point of contact — they can assess your symptoms, offer treatment, refer you to specialist services, or signpost you to community resources. NHS Talking Therapies (formerly IAPT) offers free psychological therapies for depression and anxiety and can be accessed via self-referral or GP referral. For urgent mental health concerns, you can contact the Samaritans (116 123, available 24/7), the Crisis Text Line (text SHOUT to 85258), or attend your local emergency department. For non-urgent but timely support, your GP can arrange an urgent mental health assessment. Private therapy is also available and many therapists offer sliding scale fees. Apps such as Headspace and Calm provide mindfulness tools as a complement to, but not a replacement for, professional support.

SECTION: Supporting Someone with a Mental Health Condition
Supporting a loved one with a mental health condition can be challenging but deeply valuable. Key principles include listening without judgement and validating their experience, asking how you can help rather than assuming what they need, encouraging them gently to seek professional help without applying pressure, learning about their condition so you can understand their experience better, maintaining contact and checking in regularly, and looking after your own wellbeing to avoid carer burnout. Do not trivialise their experience with phrases like "just cheer up" or "other people have it worse." Take any mention of self-harm or suicidal thoughts seriously and help them access support. Carers UK and the mental health charity Mind offer resources for those supporting someone with a mental health condition.
