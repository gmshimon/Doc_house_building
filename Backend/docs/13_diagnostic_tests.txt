SECTION: Blood Tests – Overview
Blood tests are among the most commonly performed diagnostic investigations in medicine. They are used to assess general health, diagnose conditions, monitor chronic diseases, and evaluate the effectiveness of treatments. A blood sample is typically taken from a vein in the arm using a needle and syringe. Results can usually be obtained within a few days for routine tests, or within hours for urgent investigations. Common blood test panels include the full blood count (FBC), liver function tests (LFTs), kidney function tests (renal profile), thyroid function tests (TFTs), lipid profile, blood glucose and HbA1c, inflammatory markers (CRP and ESR), and tumour markers for certain cancers. Your doctor will interpret results in the context of your symptoms, age, and medical history.

SECTION: Full Blood Count (FBC)
A full blood count measures the different types of cells in your blood: red blood cells (which carry oxygen), white blood cells (which fight infection), and platelets (which help blood clot). The FBC includes haemoglobin (to detect anaemia), haematocrit, mean corpuscular volume (MCV, which indicates the size of red blood cells and helps identify the type of anaemia), white blood cell count and differential (elevated in infection or inflammation, reduced in some conditions), and platelet count (low in thrombocytopenia, high in reactive states or myeloproliferative disorders). A low haemoglobin indicates anaemia. A high white blood cell count may indicate infection, inflammation, or rarely blood cancer. An abnormal FBC requires interpretation alongside clinical findings.

SECTION: Kidney Function Tests (Renal Profile)
Kidney function tests assess how well the kidneys are filtering waste from the blood. Key markers include creatinine (a waste product filtered by the kidneys — elevated levels suggest reduced kidney function), urea (produced from protein metabolism and filtered by the kidneys), and electrolytes including sodium, potassium, and bicarbonate. The estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) is calculated from the creatinine level and estimates how much blood the kidneys filter per minute. An eGFR below 60 for more than 3 months indicates chronic kidney disease (CKD). Kidney function tests are routinely monitored in patients with diabetes, hypertension, heart failure, and those taking medications that can affect kidney function such as NSAIDs and certain antibiotics.

SECTION: Liver Function Tests (LFTs)
Liver function tests assess the health and function of the liver. They include ALT (alanine aminotransferase) and AST (aspartate aminotransferase), which are enzymes released when liver cells are damaged and are elevated in conditions such as hepatitis, fatty liver disease, and alcohol-related liver disease. ALP (alkaline phosphatase) is elevated in liver and bile duct disease. GGT (gamma-glutamyl transferase) is often elevated in alcohol use and bile duct problems. Bilirubin, a breakdown product of haemoglobin, is elevated in liver disease and haemolytic anaemia, and causes jaundice when high. Albumin is a protein made by the liver — low albumin suggests chronic liver disease. A mildly abnormal LFT is common and may simply require repeat testing and lifestyle review.

SECTION: HbA1c – Diabetes Monitoring
HbA1c (glycated haemoglobin) is a blood test that reflects the average blood sugar level over the preceding 2 to 3 months. It is expressed as a percentage or in mmol/mol. An HbA1c below 42 mmol/mol (6%) is normal. Between 42 and 47 mmol/mol (6.0 to 6.4%) indicates prediabetes and an increased risk of type 2 diabetes. An HbA1c of 48 mmol/mol (6.5%) or above on two occasions is diagnostic of type 2 diabetes. For people with diabetes, an HbA1c target is usually set by their doctor, typically below 53 mmol/mol (7%) for most patients. The HbA1c test is used both to diagnose diabetes and to monitor how well diabetes is being managed over time.

SECTION: Thyroid Function Tests (TFTs)
Thyroid function tests assess how well the thyroid gland is working. The main tests are TSH (thyroid-stimulating hormone), T4 (thyroxine), and T3 (triiodothyronine). TSH is the most sensitive initial test: a high TSH suggests hypothyroidism (underactive thyroid) and a low TSH suggests hyperthyroidism (overactive thyroid). If TSH is abnormal, free T4 and free T3 are measured to further characterise the disorder. Hypothyroidism symptoms include fatigue, weight gain, cold intolerance, constipation, dry skin, and depression. Hyperthyroidism symptoms include weight loss, heat intolerance, palpitations, tremor, and anxiety. Thyroid antibody tests can identify autoimmune thyroid conditions such as Hashimoto's thyroiditis and Graves' disease.

SECTION: Urine Tests
Urine tests (urinalysis) are simple and informative diagnostic tools. Dipstick urinalysis tests for the presence of blood, protein, glucose, ketones, nitrites, leucocytes (white blood cells), and bilirubin. Blood in urine (haematuria) can indicate kidney stones, infection, or bladder cancer. Protein in urine (proteinuria) can indicate kidney disease or hypertension. Glucose in urine suggests diabetes. Nitrites and leucocytes suggest urinary tract infection. A urine culture is performed to identify the specific bacteria causing a UTI and determine antibiotic sensitivity. A 24-hour urine collection may be used to quantify protein loss, creatinine clearance, or hormone metabolites. Urine pregnancy tests detect the hormone hCG (human chorionic gonadotropin) and are highly accurate from the day of a missed period.

SECTION: Electrocardiogram (ECG)
An ECG records the electrical activity of the heart and is used to diagnose a wide range of cardiac conditions. It is a painless, non-invasive test that takes only a few minutes. Electrodes (small sticky patches) are attached to the chest, arms, and legs to detect electrical signals generated by the heart with each beat. An ECG can detect heart rhythm abnormalities (arrhythmias) such as atrial fibrillation, evidence of a previous or current heart attack (myocardial infarction), ventricular hypertrophy (thickening of the heart walls), conduction abnormalities, and the effects of electrolyte imbalances and medications on cardiac electrical activity. A resting ECG is a snapshot in time — conditions that come and go may require ambulatory ECG monitoring (a Holter monitor worn for 24 to 48 hours or longer).

SECTION: Imaging – X-Ray, CT, and MRI
Medical imaging allows doctors to visualise the internal structures of the body. A chest X-ray is one of the most common imaging tests and is used to assess the lungs, heart, and chest wall. It can detect pneumonia, pleural effusion, heart enlargement, and rib fractures. CT (computed tomography) scanning uses X-rays to create detailed cross-sectional images of the body. It is faster than MRI and is used in emergencies to detect brain haemorrhage, pulmonary embolism, abdominal pathology, and trauma injuries. It involves more radiation than a standard X-ray. MRI (magnetic resonance imaging) uses magnetic fields and radio waves to create detailed images without radiation. It provides superior soft tissue detail and is the preferred imaging for brain and spinal cord pathology, joint and ligament injuries, and abdominal organ assessment.

SECTION: Endoscopy and Colonoscopy
Endoscopy is a procedure that uses a thin flexible tube with a camera (endoscope) to examine internal structures of the body. Upper GI endoscopy (gastroscopy) examines the oesophagus, stomach, and first part of the small intestine. It is performed to investigate symptoms such as heartburn, difficulty swallowing, upper abdominal pain, and to detect ulcers, reflux disease, and upper GI cancers. Colonoscopy examines the entire large intestine (colon) using a longer flexible camera. It is used to investigate symptoms such as rectal bleeding, change in bowel habit, and for colorectal cancer screening in high-risk individuals. Both procedures are performed under sedation for patient comfort. Biopsies (small tissue samples) can be taken during these procedures for laboratory analysis.

SECTION: Understanding Your Test Results
When you receive test results, it is important to understand that results are interpreted in the context of your clinical history, symptoms, and other findings. Reference ranges provided on reports are based on population statistics and a small number of people without disease will have results outside the normal range. A result outside the normal range does not automatically mean something is wrong, and a result within the normal range does not necessarily mean there is no problem. Your doctor will correlate your results with your clinical picture. If you receive results you do not understand, always ask your GP or specialist to explain what they mean for your health. Online patient portals increasingly allow patients to view their own results, but results should always be discussed with a clinician for proper interpretation.
