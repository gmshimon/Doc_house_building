SECTION: Child Development Milestones
Developmental milestones are skills or behaviours that most children achieve by a certain age. They cover physical development, language and communication, cognitive development, and social and emotional skills. By 12 months, most children can sit without support, pull to stand, say simple words like "mama" and "dada", and respond to their name. By 2 years, most children can walk, run, use 2 to 3 word phrases, and follow simple instructions. By 3 years, most can climb, use 3 to 4 word sentences, and engage in pretend play. Milestones are a guide, not a rigid checklist, and children develop at different rates. If you have concerns about your child's development, speak to your GP or health visitor who can arrange a developmental assessment.

SECTION: Childhood Vaccinations Schedule
Vaccinations are one of the most effective ways to protect children from serious infectious diseases. In the UK, the childhood vaccination schedule includes: at 8, 12, and 16 weeks — 6-in-1 vaccine (diphtheria, tetanus, whooping cough, polio, Hib, hepatitis B), Men B, and rotavirus. At 12 to 13 months — Hib/Men C, MMR (measles, mumps, rubella), and Men B booster. At 3 to 4 years — 4-in-1 pre-school booster (diphtheria, tetanus, whooping cough, polio) and MMR second dose. Between 12 and 13 years — HPV vaccine. At 14 years — 3-in-1 teenage booster (tetanus, diphtheria, polio) and Men ACWY. Missing vaccinations should be discussed with a GP to arrange catch-up doses.

SECTION: Fever in Children
A fever in children is a temperature of 38 degrees Celsius or above. It is very common in children and is usually caused by a viral infection that the body is fighting. Most fevers in children are not dangerous and resolve within a few days. Signs of concern in a child with fever include age under 3 months with any fever, unusual drowsiness or difficulty waking, a rash that does not fade when pressed (which may indicate meningitis), difficulty breathing, persistent crying that cannot be soothed, seizures, fever lasting more than 5 days, and signs of dehydration. Paracetamol or ibuprofen can be used to bring down fever and relieve discomfort in children over the appropriate age. Always use the correct dose for the child's weight and age. Aspirin should never be given to children under 16.

SECTION: Common Childhood Illnesses
Children commonly experience a range of infectious illnesses. Chicken pox (varicella) causes an itchy rash of fluid-filled blisters and is spread by direct contact or respiratory droplets. Most children recover within 1 to 2 weeks. Hand, foot and mouth disease causes sores in the mouth and a rash on the hands and feet and is common in young children. Croup is a viral infection causing a distinctive barking cough, hoarseness, and breathing difficulty, particularly in children aged 6 months to 3 years. Scarlet fever presents with a red rash, sore throat, and fever and requires antibiotic treatment. Bronchiolitis is a viral infection of the small airways common in infants, causing breathing difficulty. Most mild cases can be managed at home, but breathing difficulty in an infant always requires urgent medical review.

SECTION: Asthma in Children
Asthma is one of the most common chronic conditions in children. Symptoms include wheezing, coughing (especially at night or during exercise), shortness of breath, and chest tightness. Common triggers include respiratory infections, allergens such as pollen and pet dander, cold air, exercise, and smoke. Diagnosis in young children can be difficult as other conditions can cause similar symptoms. Treatment involves reliever inhalers for acute symptoms and preventer inhalers for long-term control. An asthma action plan should be developed with your doctor and shared with the child's school. Children with poorly controlled asthma should be reviewed regularly by their GP or a paediatric respiratory specialist.

SECTION: Childhood Obesity
Childhood obesity is a serious public health concern affecting around 1 in 5 children in the UK. It increases the risk of type 2 diabetes, high blood pressure, high cholesterol, joint problems, sleep apnoea, and mental health difficulties including low self-esteem and depression. Causes include excessive consumption of high-calorie low-nutrient foods, sugary drinks, physical inactivity, excessive screen time, and genetic and socioeconomic factors. Management involves the whole family and focuses on healthier eating habits, increased physical activity, reduced screen time, and behaviour change support. Children should not be put on restrictive diets without medical supervision. The NHS offers the National Child Measurement Programme, which measures children's BMI at school ages to identify children at risk.

SECTION: Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD) in Children
ADHD is a neurodevelopmental condition characterised by persistent patterns of inattention, hyperactivity, and impulsivity that interfere with daily functioning. It is one of the most common neurodevelopmental conditions in children, affecting around 5% of school-age children. Inattention symptoms include difficulty sustaining focus, being easily distracted, forgetting daily tasks, and losing things frequently. Hyperactivity and impulsivity symptoms include fidgeting, inability to stay seated, talking excessively, and acting without thinking. Diagnosis is made by a specialist following comprehensive assessment. Treatment includes behavioural therapies, parenting support, school-based interventions, and in some cases medication such as methylphenidate (Ritalin) or lisdexamfetamine. ADHD is a long-term condition but with the right support, children can thrive.

SECTION: Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD)
Autism spectrum disorder is a neurodevelopmental condition that affects social communication, social interaction, and behaviour. It is a spectrum condition, meaning it presents differently in each individual. Common features include difficulty with social interaction and understanding social cues, challenges with verbal and non-verbal communication, repetitive behaviours or routines, strong preferences or intense interests in specific topics, and sensory sensitivities. ASD is usually identified in early childhood, though some individuals are diagnosed in adolescence or adulthood. Diagnosis involves a multi-disciplinary assessment including speech and language therapy, psychology, and paediatric input. There is no cure for ASD, but early intervention, educational support, and therapies such as speech and language therapy and occupational therapy can significantly improve outcomes.

SECTION: Nutrition for Children
Good nutrition in childhood supports healthy growth, development, and immune function. Children aged 1 to 4 need a varied diet including starchy foods (potatoes, bread, rice, pasta), fruit and vegetables (at least 5 portions per day), dairy products (milk, cheese, yoghurt) for calcium, protein (meat, fish, eggs, beans), and healthy fats. Vitamin D supplements of 10 micrograms per day are recommended for all children under 5 and for children over 5 who do not get adequate sun exposure. Iron is important for brain development and is found in red meat, lentils, fortified cereals, and leafy green vegetables. Sugary drinks and snacks should be limited. Children should not be given low-fat versions of dairy products as they need the fat for energy and development.

SECTION: Mental Health in Children and Teenagers
Mental health problems affect around 1 in 6 children and young people in the UK. Common conditions include anxiety, depression, ADHD, autism spectrum disorder, eating disorders, and self-harm. Warning signs that a child or teenager may be struggling with their mental health include persistent changes in mood or behaviour, withdrawal from friends, family, or activities, declining school performance, changes in eating or sleeping habits, unexplained physical complaints, and any talk of self-harm or feeling hopeless. Mental health problems in young people are treatable. Early intervention leads to significantly better long-term outcomes. If you are concerned about a child's mental health, speak to their GP, school nurse, or contact the Child and Adolescent Mental Health Services (CAMHS).

SECTION: When to Take Your Child to the Emergency Department
Take your child to the emergency department or call emergency services immediately if they experience: difficulty breathing or very rapid breathing, blue or grey colour around the lips, a seizure that lasts more than 5 minutes or is their first seizure, a non-blanching rash (which could indicate meningococcal disease), loss of consciousness or severe drowsiness that is difficult to rouse, a severe allergic reaction with swelling of the lips, tongue, or throat, severe abdominal pain, a head injury with loss of consciousness or persistent vomiting, signs of severe dehydration including sunken eyes, very dry mouth, no urine output for 8 hours, or a high fever in an infant under 3 months. Trust your instincts — if you are worried about your child, always seek medical attention.
