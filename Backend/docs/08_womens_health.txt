SECTION: Menstrual Health and Normal Periods
Menstruation is a natural monthly process in which the uterine lining sheds if fertilisation has not occurred. A typical menstrual cycle lasts between 21 and 35 days, with periods lasting between 2 and 7 days. Normal menstrual blood loss is between 20 and 80ml per period. It is normal to experience mild cramping, bloating, and mood changes in the days before or during a period. These are symptoms of premenstrual syndrome (PMS). A period that is significantly heavier than usual, extremely painful, irregular, or that has stopped without explanation should be discussed with a doctor. Tracking your menstrual cycle can help identify irregularities early.

SECTION: Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS)
Polycystic ovary syndrome is a common hormonal condition affecting up to 1 in 10 women of reproductive age. It is characterised by irregular or absent periods, excess androgen levels (which can cause acne, excess facial or body hair, and hair thinning on the scalp), and polycystic ovaries visible on ultrasound. PCOS is associated with insulin resistance and an increased risk of type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, and endometrial cancer. It is a leading cause of female infertility. There is no cure for PCOS, but symptoms can be managed with lifestyle changes including weight management and exercise, combined oral contraceptive pills to regulate periods, and metformin for insulin resistance. Women with PCOS who are trying to conceive may need fertility treatment.

SECTION: Endometriosis
Endometriosis is a chronic condition in which tissue similar to the uterine lining grows outside the uterus, commonly on the ovaries, fallopian tubes, and pelvic lining. It affects approximately 1 in 10 women of reproductive age. The main symptoms include severe pelvic pain, particularly during periods and intercourse, heavy periods, pain when urinating or passing stools during menstruation, and difficulty conceiving. Diagnosis often requires a laparoscopy (a minimally invasive surgical procedure). Treatment options include hormonal therapies such as the combined pill or progestins to suppress symptoms, pain management with NSAIDs, and surgical removal of endometrial tissue. Endometriosis is a leading cause of chronic pelvic pain and infertility in women.

SECTION: Cervical Screening (Smear Test)
Cervical screening is a test that checks the health of the cervix and helps prevent cervical cancer by identifying abnormal cells before they become cancerous. In the UK, women aged 25 to 49 are invited every 3 years, and women aged 50 to 64 are invited every 5 years. During the test, a sample of cells is taken from the cervix using a small brush and tested for human papillomavirus (HPV). If high-risk HPV is found, the cells are examined more closely for abnormalities. Most people with abnormal cells do not have or develop cervical cancer, but further investigation such as a colposcopy may be recommended. Attending cervical screening is one of the most important steps women can take to prevent cervical cancer.

SECTION: Breast Health and Mammography
Breast awareness involves knowing how your breasts normally look and feel so that you can identify any changes and report them to a doctor promptly. Changes to watch for include a new lump or thickening in or near the breast, changes in size, shape, or appearance of the breast, dimpling, puckering, or redness of the skin, nipple changes including discharge, inversion, or skin changes, and unexplained pain in the breast or armpit. Breast cancer screening involves a mammogram (X-ray of the breast) every 3 years for women aged 50 to 71. You should not wait for your scheduled screening if you notice a new change — see your doctor promptly. Most breast changes are not cancer, but early detection significantly improves outcomes.

SECTION: Contraception Options
Contraception prevents unwanted pregnancy. There are many methods available and the most suitable option depends on individual health, lifestyle, and reproductive goals. Short-acting methods that require regular use include the combined oral contraceptive pill (oestrogen and progestogen), the progestogen-only pill (mini pill), and condoms (which also protect against sexually transmitted infections). Long-acting reversible contraceptives (LARCs) are highly effective and include the intrauterine device (IUD or copper coil), the intrauterine system (IUS or hormonal coil), the contraceptive implant (a small rod inserted under the skin of the upper arm), and the contraceptive injection. Emergency contraception (the morning after pill or copper IUD) can be used after unprotected sex. Consult your GP or sexual health clinic for personalised contraceptive advice.

SECTION: Pregnancy – First Trimester
The first trimester covers weeks 1 to 12 of pregnancy. Common symptoms include nausea and vomiting (morning sickness), fatigue, breast tenderness, frequent urination, and mood changes. A booking appointment with a midwife should be arranged as early as possible, ideally before 10 weeks. At around 8 to 12 weeks, a dating scan confirms the pregnancy and estimates the due date. The combined screening test for Down's syndrome, Edwards' syndrome, and Patau's syndrome is offered between 11 and 14 weeks. Folic acid supplementation (400 micrograms daily) is recommended from before conception until at least 12 weeks to reduce the risk of neural tube defects. Avoid alcohol, smoking, raw or undercooked meat and eggs, unpasteurised dairy, and certain fish high in mercury during pregnancy.

SECTION: Pregnancy – Second and Third Trimester
The second trimester (weeks 13 to 27) is often called the most comfortable phase of pregnancy. Morning sickness typically improves. The anomaly scan at around 20 weeks checks for structural abnormalities in the baby. The third trimester (weeks 28 to 40) involves the baby gaining weight and preparing for birth. Common discomforts include back pain, heartburn, swollen ankles, and shortness of breath. Antenatal appointments become more frequent in the third trimester. Symptoms that require urgent medical attention during pregnancy include heavy vaginal bleeding, severe abdominal pain, reduced or absent foetal movement after 24 weeks, severe headache with vision changes (which may indicate pre-eclampsia), and leaking of amniotic fluid before 37 weeks.

SECTION: Menopause
Menopause is defined as the point at which a woman has not had a period for 12 consecutive months. It typically occurs between the ages of 45 and 55, with the average age in the UK being 51. The perimenopause is the transitional period leading up to menopause, which can last several years. Common symptoms include hot flushes, night sweats, irregular periods, vaginal dryness, mood changes, difficulty sleeping, reduced sex drive, and problems with memory and concentration. Symptoms vary widely in severity. Treatment options include hormone replacement therapy (HRT), which is the most effective treatment for menopausal symptoms and also protects bone health, as well as non-hormonal options and lifestyle modifications. Women should discuss the benefits and risks of HRT with their doctor.

SECTION: Urinary Incontinence in Women
Urinary incontinence is the involuntary leakage of urine. It is far more common in women than men and becomes more prevalent with age. Stress incontinence involves leakage during physical activity that increases abdominal pressure, such as coughing, sneezing, laughing, or exercising, and is caused by weakness of the pelvic floor muscles. Urge incontinence involves a sudden, intense urge to urinate followed by involuntary leakage, and is associated with an overactive bladder. Mixed incontinence is a combination of both. Risk factors include pregnancy, childbirth, menopause, obesity, and chronic cough. First-line treatment includes pelvic floor exercises (Kegel exercises), bladder training, and lifestyle modifications such as reducing caffeine and maintaining a healthy weight. Medical or surgical treatments are available for more severe cases.

SECTION: Osteoporosis in Women
Women are at significantly higher risk of osteoporosis than men, particularly after menopause, because of the decline in oestrogen which plays a key role in maintaining bone density. Osteoporosis is often asymptomatic until a fracture occurs. The most common fracture sites are the hip, wrist, and spine. A DEXA scan (dual-energy X-ray absorptiometry) measures bone density and is recommended for women at risk, including those who have had an early menopause, have a family history of hip fracture, or have been on long-term corticosteroids. Prevention and treatment include adequate calcium and vitamin D intake, weight-bearing exercise, avoiding smoking and excess alcohol, and medications such as bisphosphonates or HRT in appropriate cases.
