SECTION: Men's Health Overview
Men are statistically less likely than women to seek medical help or attend regular health check-ups, which contributes to poorer health outcomes across many conditions. On average, men in the UK live around 4 years less than women. Major health threats to men include cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, prostate cancer, testicular cancer, mental health conditions, and lung disease. Many of these conditions are preventable or treatable when detected early. Men should attend regular health checks, be aware of warning signs in their bodies, and not delay seeking medical attention for new or concerning symptoms. GP practices offer NHS Health Checks to adults aged 40 to 74 which screen for common risk factors.

SECTION: Prostate Health and Prostate Cancer
The prostate is a small walnut-sized gland below the bladder that produces seminal fluid. Prostate cancer is the most common cancer in men in the UK, with around 52,000 new cases diagnosed each year. It most commonly affects men over 50. Risk factors include age, family history, and being of Black African or Caribbean descent (which carries a 2 to 3 times higher risk). Early prostate cancer often has no symptoms. Advanced disease may cause urinary symptoms such as needing to urinate more frequently, difficulty starting urination, weak flow, and blood in the urine or semen. A PSA (prostate-specific antigen) blood test can indicate prostate problems but is not a definitive cancer test. Men concerned about prostate cancer can request a PSA test from their GP. Treatment options include active surveillance, surgery, radiotherapy, and hormone therapy depending on the stage and grade of cancer.

SECTION: Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia (BPH)
Benign prostatic hyperplasia is a non-cancerous enlargement of the prostate gland that is very common in men over 50. The enlarged prostate can press on the urethra, causing urinary symptoms including frequent urination (especially at night), difficulty starting or stopping urination, a weak urine stream, a feeling of incomplete bladder emptying, and in some cases urinary retention. BPH is not cancer and does not increase the risk of prostate cancer. Mild symptoms can be managed with lifestyle changes such as reducing fluid intake before bed and limiting caffeine and alcohol. Medical treatments include alpha-blockers (which relax the muscles of the prostate) and 5-alpha reductase inhibitors (which shrink the prostate). Surgical options are available for more severe cases.

SECTION: Testicular Cancer
Testicular cancer is the most common cancer in men aged 15 to 49. Despite this, it has a very high cure rate of over 95% when detected early. The most common symptom is a painless lump or swelling in one of the testicles. Other symptoms include a dull ache or feeling of heaviness in the scrotum, a change in size or shape of a testicle, and occasionally breast tenderness due to hormonal changes caused by the tumour. Men should perform regular self-examinations of the testicles and report any new lumps, changes, or symptoms to a doctor promptly. Treatment typically involves surgical removal of the affected testicle (orchidectomy) followed by chemotherapy or radiotherapy depending on the type and stage of cancer.

SECTION: Erectile Dysfunction
Erectile dysfunction (ED) is the inability to get or maintain an erection sufficient for satisfactory sexual activity. It is a common condition that affects up to 50% of men over 40 to some degree. ED can have physical causes including cardiovascular disease, diabetes, hypertension, hormonal imbalances, and neurological conditions, as well as psychological causes including stress, anxiety, depression, and relationship problems. ED is often an early warning sign of cardiovascular disease, as narrowing of the penile arteries may precede coronary artery disease. Men with ED should not ignore it but rather discuss it with their GP who can investigate underlying causes. Treatment options include lifestyle changes, oral medications (PDE5 inhibitors such as sildenafil), psychological therapy, and in some cases surgical devices.

SECTION: Male Mental Health
Men are less likely to seek help for mental health problems than women, but are significantly more likely to die by suicide. In the UK, suicide is the leading cause of death in men under 50. Societal expectations around masculinity can create barriers to men acknowledging emotional difficulties and seeking support. Common mental health conditions in men include depression, anxiety, and substance misuse. Symptoms of depression in men may present differently from the clinical stereotype and can include irritability, aggression, increased alcohol use, risk-taking behaviour, and physical symptoms such as headaches or digestive problems. If you are struggling with your mental health, speak to your GP, a trusted person in your life, or contact a crisis service. Seeking help is a sign of strength, not weakness.

SECTION: Cardiovascular Risk in Men
Men have a higher risk of cardiovascular disease than women, particularly before the age of 65. Risk factors include high blood pressure, high cholesterol, smoking, type 2 diabetes, obesity, physical inactivity, excessive alcohol consumption, and a family history of heart disease. Cardiovascular disease is the leading cause of death in men worldwide. The risk can be significantly reduced through lifestyle modifications including stopping smoking, eating a heart-healthy diet, exercising regularly, maintaining a healthy weight, limiting alcohol, and managing stress. Regular blood pressure and cholesterol checks are important for early identification of risk. Men with multiple risk factors may be prescribed preventive medications such as statins and antihypertensives.

SECTION: Male Infertility
Male infertility is a factor in approximately half of all cases of infertility in couples. The most common cause is low sperm count (oligospermia), poor sperm motility (movement), and abnormal sperm morphology (shape). Other causes include hormonal imbalances, varicocele (enlarged veins in the testicle), previous infections such as mumps orchitis, certain medications, smoking, excessive alcohol, obesity, and exposure to heat or environmental toxins. A semen analysis is the first investigation for male infertility. Depending on the cause, treatments include lifestyle modifications, medications to address hormonal imbalances, surgical correction of varicocele, and assisted reproductive techniques such as IVF with intracytoplasmic sperm injection (ICSI).

SECTION: Hair Loss in Men
Male pattern baldness (androgenetic alopecia) is the most common cause of hair loss in men, affecting around half of all men by the age of 50. It is caused by a combination of genetic factors and the hormone dihydrotestosterone (DHT), which causes hair follicles to shrink over time. Hair loss typically begins at the temples or crown and progresses gradually. Other causes of hair loss include stress (telogen effluvium), nutritional deficiencies, thyroid disorders, certain medications, and alopecia areata (an autoimmune condition causing patchy hair loss). Medical treatments for male pattern baldness include topical minoxidil and oral finasteride. Hair transplant surgery is an option for more significant hair loss. If hair loss is sudden, patchy, or accompanied by other symptoms, see a doctor.

SECTION: Type 2 Diabetes Risk in Men
Men are at higher risk of developing type 2 diabetes than women at a given BMI, partly because men tend to store more fat around the abdomen (visceral fat), which is more metabolically harmful. Waist circumference is an important risk indicator — a waist measurement above 94cm in men increases diabetes risk, and above 102cm indicates high risk. In addition to the general risk factors for type 2 diabetes, testosterone deficiency in men is associated with insulin resistance and increased diabetes risk. Regular blood glucose screening is recommended for men over 40 with risk factors. Lifestyle changes including weight loss, dietary improvement, and regular exercise can significantly reduce the risk of developing type 2 diabetes or delay its onset.
