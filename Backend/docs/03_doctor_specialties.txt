SECTION: General Practitioner (GP) / Family Doctor
A General Practitioner is a doctor who treats patients of all ages for a wide range of health conditions. GPs are usually the first point of contact in the healthcare system. They diagnose and manage both acute and chronic illnesses, provide preventive care, perform routine check-ups, prescribe medications, and refer patients to specialists when necessary. GPs also provide continuity of care, managing long-term conditions such as diabetes, hypertension, and asthma. You should see a GP for general health concerns, routine check-ups, vaccinations, minor injuries, mental health concerns, and when you are unsure which specialist you need.

SECTION: Cardiologist
A cardiologist is a doctor who specialises in the diagnosis, treatment, and prevention of diseases of the heart and blood vessels. Cardiologists manage conditions including coronary artery disease, heart failure, arrhythmias (irregular heartbeats), hypertension, and valvular heart disease. They perform and interpret diagnostic tests such as electrocardiograms (ECGs), echocardiograms, stress tests, and cardiac catheterisation. Interventional cardiologists perform procedures such as angioplasty and stent placement. You should see a cardiologist if you have chest pain, palpitations, shortness of breath, or a history of heart disease. You may be referred by your GP after an abnormal ECG or elevated cardiac risk.

SECTION: Dermatologist
A dermatologist is a specialist in conditions affecting the skin, hair, and nails. They diagnose and treat over 3000 conditions including acne, eczema, psoriasis, rosacea, skin infections, hair loss, and nail disorders. Dermatologists also perform skin cancer screenings and biopsies. Cosmetic dermatology includes procedures such as Botox, fillers, laser therapy, and chemical peels. You should see a dermatologist if you have a persistent skin condition that is not responding to over-the-counter treatments, a mole or lesion that has changed in size, shape, or colour, unexplained hair loss, or chronic nail problems.

SECTION: Neurologist
A neurologist is a doctor who specialises in disorders of the brain, spinal cord, nerves, and muscles. Common conditions managed by neurologists include migraines, epilepsy, stroke, multiple sclerosis, Parkinson's disease, dementia, and peripheral neuropathy. Neurologists use diagnostic tools such as MRI scans, CT scans, EEG (electroencephalogram), and nerve conduction studies. You should see a neurologist if you experience persistent severe headaches, seizures, memory problems, tremors, weakness or numbness in the limbs, difficulty walking, or sudden vision changes. Stroke symptoms require emergency services, not a scheduled neurologist appointment.

SECTION: Orthopaedic Surgeon
An orthopaedic surgeon specialises in the musculoskeletal system, which includes bones, joints, ligaments, tendons, and muscles. They diagnose and treat fractures, dislocations, arthritis, sports injuries, spinal disorders, and congenital conditions. Common procedures include joint replacement (hip and knee), arthroscopy, spinal surgery, and fracture repair. Not all orthopaedic consultations lead to surgery; many conditions are managed with physiotherapy, medication, or injections. You should see an orthopaedic surgeon if you have persistent joint pain, limited range of motion, a sports injury that is not healing, or a fracture requiring surgical fixation.

SECTION: Gynaecologist
A gynaecologist is a doctor who specialises in the female reproductive system, including the uterus, ovaries, fallopian tubes, cervix, and vagina. They manage conditions such as menstrual disorders, polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS), endometriosis, uterine fibroids, pelvic inflammatory disease, and cervical cancer. Gynaecologists perform procedures including cervical smear tests (Pap smears), colposcopies, hysteroscopies, and hysterectomies. An obstetrician is a gynaecologist who specialises in pregnancy, labour, and childbirth. Women should see a gynaecologist for irregular or painful periods, abnormal vaginal discharge, pelvic pain, concerns about fertility, or routine cervical screening.

SECTION: Psychiatrist
A psychiatrist is a medical doctor who specialises in the diagnosis, treatment, and prevention of mental health disorders. Unlike psychologists, psychiatrists can prescribe medication. They manage conditions including depression, anxiety disorders, bipolar disorder, schizophrenia, eating disorders, obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD), and post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD). Treatment approaches include medication management, psychotherapy, and in severe cases, hospitalisation or electroconvulsive therapy (ECT). You should see a psychiatrist if you have a mental health condition requiring medication, complex or severe symptoms, or if psychological therapy alone has not been sufficient.

SECTION: Gastroenterologist
A gastroenterologist is a specialist in the digestive system, which includes the oesophagus, stomach, small intestine, large intestine, liver, gallbladder, and pancreas. They diagnose and treat conditions such as irritable bowel syndrome (IBS), Crohn's disease, ulcerative colitis, acid reflux (GERD), peptic ulcers, liver disease, and colorectal cancer. Key procedures performed include endoscopy (examining the oesophagus and stomach), colonoscopy (examining the large intestine), and liver biopsy. You should see a gastroenterologist if you have persistent abdominal pain, blood in your stool, unexplained weight loss, chronic diarrhoea or constipation, difficulty swallowing, or abnormal liver function tests.

SECTION: Endocrinologist
An endocrinologist is a doctor who specialises in the endocrine system, which includes glands and hormones that regulate metabolism, growth, reproduction, and mood. Common conditions managed by endocrinologists include diabetes (Type 1 and Type 2), thyroid disorders (hypothyroidism, hyperthyroidism, thyroid nodules), adrenal disorders, pituitary disorders, osteoporosis, and polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS). You should see an endocrinologist if your diabetes or thyroid condition is difficult to control, if you have unexplained weight gain or loss, unusual fatigue, or hormonal imbalances that your GP is unable to manage effectively.

SECTION: Pulmonologist (Respiratory Physician)
A pulmonologist is a specialist in the lungs and respiratory system. They diagnose and treat conditions including asthma, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), pneumonia, tuberculosis, pulmonary fibrosis, sleep apnoea, and lung cancer. Diagnostic tools used include spirometry (breathing tests), bronchoscopy, chest X-rays, CT scans, and sleep studies. You should see a pulmonologist if you have a persistent cough lasting more than 8 weeks, unexplained breathlessness, wheezing, frequent chest infections, abnormal chest imaging, or snoring and suspected sleep apnoea.

SECTION: Ophthalmologist
An ophthalmologist is a medical doctor specialising in the diagnosis and treatment of eye and vision disorders. They are distinct from optometrists, who primarily conduct eye exams and prescribe glasses. Ophthalmologists manage conditions such as cataracts, glaucoma, macular degeneration, diabetic retinopathy, retinal detachment, and strabismus (crossed eyes). They perform surgical procedures including cataract surgery, LASIK, and retinal surgery. You should see an ophthalmologist if you experience sudden vision loss, eye pain, flashes of light, floaters, double vision, or if you have diabetes or hypertension that may affect your eyes.

SECTION: Urologist
A urologist is a specialist in the urinary tract and male reproductive system. They manage conditions including kidney stones, urinary tract infections (UTIs), urinary incontinence, prostate disease (including benign prostatic hyperplasia and prostate cancer), bladder cancer, erectile dysfunction, and infertility in men. Common procedures include cystoscopy (examining the bladder), kidney stone removal, and prostate surgery. You should see a urologist if you have blood in the urine, pain during urination, frequent or urgent urination, difficulty urinating, pelvic or lower abdominal pain, or male fertility concerns.

SECTION: ENT Specialist (Otolaryngologist)
An ENT specialist, or otolaryngologist, focuses on conditions affecting the ear, nose, and throat. They treat conditions such as hearing loss, tinnitus, ear infections, sinusitis, tonsillitis, nasal polyps, deviated septum, sleep apnoea, and head and neck cancers. Procedures include tonsillectomy, adenoidectomy, ear tube insertion, sinus surgery, and cochlear implant placement. You should see an ENT specialist if you have recurring ear infections, persistent sinus problems, hearing loss, chronic sore throat, difficulty swallowing, a lump in the neck, or voice changes that do not resolve.
