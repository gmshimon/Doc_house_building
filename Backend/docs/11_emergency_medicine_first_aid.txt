SECTION: Recognising a Medical Emergency
A medical emergency is a sudden, potentially life-threatening situation requiring immediate professional medical intervention. Always call emergency services (999 in the UK, 911 in the US, 112 in Europe) for the following situations: chest pain that may indicate a heart attack, sudden severe headache described as the worst of your life, signs of stroke (face drooping, arm weakness, speech difficulty), difficulty breathing, loss of consciousness, severe allergic reaction (anaphylaxis), uncontrolled bleeding, suspected spinal injury, seizures lasting more than 5 minutes, severe burns, suspected poisoning or overdose, and any situation where a person's life may be at immediate risk. Do not drive yourself or the patient to hospital in a life-threatening emergency — call for an ambulance.

SECTION: Heart Attack – Signs and First Aid
A heart attack occurs when blood supply to part of the heart muscle is blocked, usually by a blood clot. Classic symptoms include central crushing chest pain or pressure that may spread to the left arm, jaw, neck, or back, shortness of breath, sweating, nausea, and a feeling of impending doom. Some heart attacks, particularly in women, diabetics, and the elderly, may present atypically with fatigue, indigestion-like symptoms, or jaw pain without classic chest pain. If a heart attack is suspected: call emergency services immediately, help the person sit or lie down in a comfortable position, loosen any tight clothing, give 300mg aspirin to chew (if available and not allergic), and be prepared to perform CPR if the person loses consciousness and stops breathing normally.

SECTION: Stroke – FAST Recognition and First Aid
A stroke occurs when blood supply to the brain is interrupted, either by a blocked artery (ischaemic stroke, accounting for 85% of strokes) or a burst blood vessel (haemorrhagic stroke). The FAST acronym is the key recognition tool: Face drooping (is one side of the face drooping or numb, ask the person to smile and check for asymmetry), Arm weakness (is one arm weak or numb, ask the person to raise both arms and see if one drifts downward), Speech difficulty (is speech slurred, garbled, or is the person unable to speak or be understood), Time to call emergency services immediately. Other symptoms of stroke include sudden severe headache with no known cause, sudden vision problems in one or both eyes, sudden dizziness or loss of balance. Every minute counts in stroke treatment — do not wait to see if symptoms improve.

SECTION: Anaphylaxis – Recognition and Treatment
Anaphylaxis is a severe, potentially life-threatening allergic reaction that can occur within seconds to minutes of exposure to an allergen. Common triggers include certain foods (peanuts, tree nuts, shellfish, milk, eggs), insect stings, medications (particularly penicillin and NSAIDs), and latex. Symptoms include sudden onset of swelling of the lips, tongue, or throat, difficulty breathing or swallowing, a drop in blood pressure causing dizziness or collapse, rapid or weak pulse, skin rash or hives, nausea and vomiting, and loss of consciousness. Treatment is urgent injection of adrenaline (epinephrine), typically using an auto-injector (such as an EpiPen). Call emergency services immediately even if symptoms improve after adrenaline. The person should be laid flat with legs raised unless they have difficulty breathing. Further doses of adrenaline may be needed.

SECTION: Choking – First Aid
Choking occurs when a foreign object becomes lodged in the throat or windpipe, blocking airflow. Mild choking: encourage the person to continue coughing, which is the most effective way to clear the airway. Severe choking (person cannot speak, cry, cough effectively, or breathe): give up to 5 sharp back blows between the shoulder blades using the heel of your hand. If back blows are ineffective, give up to 5 abdominal thrusts (Heimlich manoeuvre): stand behind the person, place a fist just above the navel, grasp with the other hand, and pull sharply inward and upward. Alternate 5 back blows and 5 abdominal thrusts until the object is dislodged or emergency services arrive. For infants under 1 year, use back blows and chest thrusts, not abdominal thrusts.

SECTION: Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation (CPR)
CPR is an emergency procedure used when a person is unresponsive and not breathing normally. It keeps oxygenated blood circulating to the brain and vital organs until professional medical help arrives. Adult CPR: check for danger, tap shoulders and shout to check responsiveness, if unresponsive call emergency services or ask someone to call, tilt head back and lift chin, look listen and feel for normal breathing, if not breathing normally, begin chest compressions: place heel of hand on centre of chest, interlock fingers, compress at least 5cm deep at a rate of 100 to 120 per minute, allow full chest recoil. After 30 compressions, give 2 rescue breaths if trained and willing. Continue cycles of 30 compressions to 2 breaths until help arrives, an AED is available, or the person recovers. Hands-only CPR (compressions only) is acceptable if you are not trained in rescue breaths.

SECTION: Using an Automated External Defibrillator (AED)
An AED is a portable device that delivers an electric shock to restore normal heart rhythm in a person experiencing a sudden cardiac arrest. AEDs are found in many public places including airports, shopping centres, train stations, and sports venues. They are designed to be used by non-medical bystanders. To use an AED: turn it on (the machine will provide voice instructions), attach the pads to the bare chest as shown in the diagram, allow the machine to analyse the heart rhythm, if a shock is advised, ensure nobody is touching the patient and press the shock button, immediately resume CPR after the shock. Early defibrillation significantly increases the chance of survival from cardiac arrest. Every minute without defibrillation reduces survival chances by approximately 10%.

SECTION: Severe Bleeding – First Aid
Severe bleeding can be life-threatening. First aid for severe bleeding: apply direct pressure to the wound using a clean cloth, bandage, or clothing. Maintain firm, continuous pressure and do not remove the material if it becomes soaked — add more material on top. If possible, elevate the injured limb above the level of the heart. For limb bleeding that cannot be controlled by direct pressure, a tourniquet applied 5 to 7cm above the wound can be life-saving. Note the time the tourniquet is applied. Do not remove a tourniquet once applied — leave this to medical professionals. If an object is embedded in the wound, do not remove it — apply pressure around it. Call emergency services for severe or uncontrolled bleeding.

SECTION: Burns and Scalds – First Aid
Burns are injuries to the skin caused by heat, chemicals, electricity, or radiation. A scald is a burn caused by hot liquid or steam. First aid for burns: immediately cool the burn under cool (not cold) running water for at least 20 minutes. Do not use ice, butter, toothpaste, or any other substance. Remove jewellery and clothing from around the burn unless it is stuck to the skin. Cover the burn loosely with cling film or a clean non-fluffy material. Do not burst blisters. Seek medical attention for burns larger than the size of the patient's palm, burns on the face, hands, feet, genitals, or joints, chemical or electrical burns, and any burn in a child or elderly person. Call emergency services for large or severe burns.

SECTION: Seizures – First Aid
A seizure is a sudden, uncontrolled electrical disturbance in the brain. During a convulsive seizure: do not restrain the person or put anything in their mouth. Clear the area around them of hard or sharp objects. Place something soft under their head and protect it from injury. Time the seizure. After the convulsive movements stop, place the person in the recovery position (on their side) to keep the airway clear. Stay with them until they are fully recovered. Call emergency services if: the seizure lasts more than 5 minutes, a second seizure follows shortly after, the person does not regain consciousness after the seizure stops, the person is injured, is pregnant, or has diabetes, or it is the person's first known seizure.

SECTION: Poisoning and Overdose – First Aid
Poisoning occurs when a harmful substance is swallowed, inhaled, injected, or absorbed through the skin. Common causes include medications taken in excess, household chemicals, alcohol, and illicit drugs. If poisoning or overdose is suspected: call emergency services immediately. Try to identify what was taken, how much, and when. Do not induce vomiting unless specifically instructed by emergency services, as this can cause additional harm with some substances. If the person is unconscious but breathing, place them in the recovery position. If they are not breathing, begin CPR. Keep any packaging, bottles, or substances to show the medical team. Never leave a person who has taken an overdose alone.
