SECTION: How to Choose the Right Doctor
Choosing the right doctor depends on the nature of your health concern. For general health issues, routine check-ups, vaccinations, and initial assessments of new symptoms, a General Practitioner (GP) or family doctor is the correct first step. GPs can diagnose and treat the majority of health conditions and will refer you to a specialist if needed. If you have been previously diagnosed with a condition requiring specialist care, such as a heart condition or diabetes that is difficult to control, you may need to book directly with a specialist. When in doubt, always start with your GP as they can guide you to the right specialist.

SECTION: What to Expect at Your First Appointment
At your first appointment with a new doctor, you can expect the consultation to last between 15 and 30 minutes. The doctor will ask about your current symptoms, your medical history including past illnesses, surgeries, and hospitalisations, your family medical history, your current medications and allergies, and your lifestyle habits such as smoking, alcohol, diet, and exercise. It is helpful to prepare a list of your symptoms and questions beforehand. Bring any relevant documents such as previous test results, referral letters, or a list of your current medications. Be open and honest with your doctor as this helps them make an accurate diagnosis.

SECTION: Routine vs Urgent Appointments
A routine appointment is for non-urgent health matters such as follow-up consultations, prescription renewals, health check-ups, management of stable chronic conditions, and vaccinations. These appointments can typically be booked in advance and scheduled at your convenience. An urgent appointment is required when you have a sudden or worsening health issue that needs to be assessed within 24 to 48 hours but is not life-threatening. Examples include a high fever, a new rash, worsening pain, or an infection. An emergency requires immediate medical attention. Call emergency services or go to the nearest emergency department for symptoms such as chest pain, difficulty breathing, sudden severe headache, signs of stroke, or serious injury.

SECTION: How to Prepare for a Specialist Appointment
Specialist appointments are typically shorter and more focused than GP consultations. To make the most of your appointment, bring your referral letter from your GP, a list of your current medications and dosages, any relevant previous test results or imaging reports, and a written list of your symptoms including when they started, how severe they are, and what makes them better or worse. Write down any questions you want to ask the specialist. If you have had similar symptoms before, note what treatment was tried and whether it helped. Some specialist appointments may require you to have fasting blood tests or other preparations beforehand — check with the clinic when booking.

SECTION: Understanding Referrals
A referral is a recommendation from your GP for you to see a specialist for a specific health concern. Referrals are made when your condition requires more specialised knowledge, diagnostic equipment, or treatment than a GP can provide. A referral letter contains a summary of your medical history, your current symptoms, relevant test results, and the reason for referral. Some healthcare systems allow self-referral to certain specialties such as ophthalmology or physiotherapy. In most cases, you should contact the specialist's clinic after receiving your referral to book your appointment. Urgent referrals are prioritised and usually result in an appointment within days to weeks, while routine referrals may take longer.

SECTION: Follow-up Appointments
Follow-up appointments are scheduled after an initial consultation or treatment to monitor progress, review test results, adjust medications, or assess recovery. It is important to attend all follow-up appointments as they allow your doctor to detect complications early and ensure your treatment plan is working. If you need to cancel or reschedule, do so as early as possible so the appointment can be given to another patient. Between appointments, keep a diary of your symptoms, noting any changes, new symptoms, or side effects from medication. This information is valuable for your doctor during the follow-up.

SECTION: Telemedicine and Online Appointments
Telemedicine allows patients to consult with a doctor via video call, phone, or online messaging without visiting a clinic in person. It is suitable for consultations that do not require a physical examination, such as discussing test results, medication reviews, mental health check-ins, prescription renewals, and follow-up appointments for stable conditions. Telemedicine is not appropriate for emergencies, new or unexplained symptoms that may require examination, or conditions requiring diagnostic tests or procedures. Online appointments are convenient, reduce travel time, and are particularly beneficial for patients in remote areas or those with mobility limitations. Always use a secure and reputable platform for telemedicine consultations.

SECTION: What Information to Provide When Booking
When booking a doctor's appointment, you will typically need to provide your full name and date of birth, your contact number and email address, the reason for your appointment (a brief description of your symptoms or concern), whether it is a new or follow-up appointment, your preferred date and time, and any accessibility requirements. Providing an accurate reason for your appointment helps the clinic allocate the correct amount of time and ensures you see the most appropriate doctor. For new patients, you may also be asked to provide insurance details, your GP's name, and a brief medical history.

SECTION: Cancellation and Rescheduling Policy
If you need to cancel or reschedule your appointment, it is important to do so as soon as possible. Most clinics require at least 24 to 48 hours notice for cancellations. Late cancellations or missed appointments (no-shows) deprive other patients of the opportunity to be seen and may result in a cancellation fee. If you are running late for your appointment, call the clinic to let them know. They may be able to accommodate you or reschedule. Repeated missed appointments without notice may result in the clinic removing you from their patient list. If you need to cancel urgently due to an emergency, most clinics will waive any cancellation fees with a reasonable explanation.

SECTION: Symptom Checker and When to Use It
An online symptom checker can help you decide whether you need to see a doctor, which type of doctor to see, and how urgently you need to be seen. You enter your symptoms and the tool provides guidance based on your inputs. Symptom checkers are useful as a first step but are not a substitute for professional medical advice. They cannot examine you, do not have access to your medical history, and may not account for rare conditions. Always consult a doctor if you are unsure about your symptoms, if your symptoms are worsening, or if a symptom checker recommends seeking medical attention. In an emergency, do not use a symptom checker — call emergency services immediately.
