SECTION: What is Preventive Healthcare
Preventive healthcare refers to measures taken to prevent diseases, rather than treating them after they occur. It is divided into three levels. Primary prevention aims to prevent disease before it develops, such as through vaccinations, healthy diet, and exercise. Secondary prevention involves early detection of disease through screening programmes such as cancer screenings or blood pressure checks, enabling earlier and more effective treatment. Tertiary prevention focuses on managing existing disease to prevent complications and improve quality of life. Preventive healthcare reduces the burden on health systems and improves life expectancy. It is one of the most cost-effective approaches to long-term public health.

SECTION: Healthy Diet and Nutrition
A healthy diet provides the nutrients the body needs to function correctly. Key components include a variety of fruits and vegetables (at least 5 portions per day), whole grains such as brown rice, oats, and wholegrain bread, lean proteins including fish, poultry, legumes, and nuts, healthy fats from sources like olive oil, avocado, and oily fish, and limited intake of processed foods, added sugars, and saturated fats. Salt intake should be below 6 grams per day to reduce the risk of hypertension. Red meat should be limited and processed meats such as sausages and bacon should be avoided as much as possible. A Mediterranean-style diet is widely recognised as one of the most beneficial dietary patterns for long-term cardiovascular and metabolic health.

SECTION: Physical Activity and Exercise
Regular physical activity is one of the most powerful tools for maintaining health and preventing chronic disease. Adults should aim for at least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic activity per week, such as brisk walking, cycling, or swimming, or 75 minutes of vigorous-intensity activity such as running. Muscle-strengthening activities should be done at least twice a week. Physical activity reduces the risk of cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, certain cancers, and mental health conditions including depression and anxiety. It helps maintain a healthy weight, improves sleep quality, strengthens bones, and increases energy levels. Even small amounts of physical activity are beneficial, and any increase in activity level is better than none.

SECTION: Smoking and Health
Smoking is the leading cause of preventable death worldwide. It causes lung cancer, mouth cancer, throat cancer, oesophageal cancer, bladder cancer, and kidney cancer. Smoking also causes chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), heart disease, stroke, and peripheral arterial disease. It damages nearly every organ in the body. Secondhand smoke also poses serious risks to non-smokers, particularly children. Quitting smoking at any age has significant health benefits. Within 20 minutes of quitting, blood pressure and heart rate drop. Within 12 hours, carbon monoxide levels normalise. Within a year, the risk of heart disease is halved. Support for quitting includes nicotine replacement therapy, prescription medications such as varenicline, and behavioural support programmes.

SECTION: Alcohol and Health
Alcohol is a depressant that affects the brain, liver, heart, and other organs. The recommended limit for adults is no more than 14 units of alcohol per week, spread over at least 3 days. One unit is equivalent to half a pint of regular beer, a small glass of wine, or a single measure of spirits. Excessive alcohol consumption increases the risk of liver disease (including cirrhosis and liver cancer), pancreatitis, heart disease, stroke, several types of cancer, depression, and dependency. Binge drinking, defined as consuming more than 6 units in a single session, is particularly harmful. Alcohol should be avoided entirely during pregnancy. If you are concerned about your alcohol intake, speak to your doctor about support options.

SECTION: Mental Health and Wellbeing
Mental health is an integral part of overall health and wellbeing. It encompasses emotional, psychological, and social wellbeing and affects how we think, feel, and act. Good mental health is not merely the absence of mental illness but includes the ability to cope with stress, maintain relationships, and lead a fulfilling life. Protective factors include regular physical activity, strong social connections, adequate sleep, a sense of purpose, and access to mental health support when needed. Common mental health challenges include stress, anxiety, depression, and burnout. Seeking help early is important. Talking therapies such as cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) are effective for many conditions. There is no shame in seeking help for mental health concerns.

SECTION: Cancer Screening and Early Detection
Cancer screening programmes detect cancer or precancerous conditions before symptoms develop, significantly improving treatment outcomes. Breast cancer screening involves a mammogram every 3 years for women aged 50 to 71. Cervical cancer screening (smear test) is recommended every 3 to 5 years for women aged 25 to 64. Bowel cancer screening is offered every 2 years to adults aged 60 to 74 using a home stool sample test. Prostate-specific antigen (PSA) testing is available for men who request it, particularly those over 50 with risk factors. Lung cancer screening with low-dose CT scans is recommended for high-risk individuals such as current or former heavy smokers. Attending all recommended screenings is one of the most important steps you can take to protect your health.

SECTION: Bone Health and Osteoporosis Prevention
Bones reach their maximum density in early adulthood and gradually lose density with age. Osteoporosis is a condition in which bones become weak and brittle, increasing the risk of fractures. Risk factors include being female, older age, low body weight, family history, low calcium intake, vitamin D deficiency, smoking, excessive alcohol, and long-term steroid use. To protect bone health, ensure adequate calcium intake (700mg per day for adults) from dairy products, fortified foods, and leafy green vegetables. Vitamin D is essential for calcium absorption and can be obtained from sunlight, oily fish, eggs, and supplements (10 micrograms per day is recommended in autumn and winter). Weight-bearing exercise such as walking, dancing, and resistance training helps maintain bone density.

SECTION: Eye Health and Vision Care
Regular eye examinations are important for maintaining good vision and detecting eye conditions early. Adults should have an eye test at least every 2 years. Eye tests can detect conditions such as glaucoma, macular degeneration, cataracts, and diabetic retinopathy, as well as systemic conditions like hypertension and diabetes. Protecting your eyes from UV light by wearing sunglasses outdoors reduces the risk of cataracts and macular degeneration. If you use screens for extended periods, follow the 20-20-20 rule: every 20 minutes, look at something 20 feet away for at least 20 seconds. Eat a diet rich in leafy green vegetables, oily fish, and foods containing vitamins C and E to support long-term eye health.

SECTION: Oral Health
Good oral health is closely linked to overall health. Poor oral hygiene is associated with an increased risk of heart disease, diabetes complications, respiratory infections, and adverse pregnancy outcomes. Teeth should be brushed twice daily with fluoride toothpaste for at least 2 minutes. Flossing or using interdental brushes daily helps remove plaque between teeth. Limit sugary foods and drinks, particularly between meals. Regular dental check-ups, at least once every 1 to 2 years, allow dentists to detect cavities, gum disease, and oral cancer early. Gum disease (periodontitis) is a common condition that, if left untreated, can lead to tooth loss and has been linked to cardiovascular disease.

SECTION: Stress Management
Chronic stress has significant negative effects on physical and mental health. It is associated with increased risk of heart disease, high blood pressure, digestive problems, sleep disorders, weakened immune function, and mental health conditions. Effective stress management strategies include regular physical activity, mindfulness and meditation practices, adequate sleep, time management, maintaining social connections, and seeking professional support when needed. Deep breathing exercises and progressive muscle relaxation can provide immediate relief during stressful situations. Identifying sources of stress and making practical changes where possible is an important long-term strategy. If stress is significantly impacting your quality of life, speak to your GP or a mental health professional.

SECTION: Healthy Ageing
Healthy ageing involves maintaining physical, mental, and social wellbeing as we grow older. Key strategies include staying physically active with a combination of aerobic exercise, strength training, and balance exercises to reduce fall risk. Eating a nutritious diet supports energy levels, bone health, and immune function. Regular health check-ups become increasingly important with age. Staying socially connected reduces the risk of loneliness, depression, and cognitive decline. Mental stimulation through reading, puzzles, learning new skills, and creative activities supports brain health. Managing chronic conditions such as hypertension, diabetes, and arthritis with the help of your healthcare team is essential for maintaining quality of life in older age.
