SECTION: Living with a Chronic Condition
A chronic condition is a health problem that persists for 12 months or more and requires ongoing medical management. Common chronic conditions include type 2 diabetes, hypertension, asthma, COPD, heart disease, chronic kidney disease, arthritis, and depression. Living with a chronic condition involves a partnership between the patient and their healthcare team. Self-management — the ability to manage symptoms, treatment, lifestyle changes, and the physical and psychosocial impact of a condition — is central to good outcomes. Key principles of successful chronic disease management include adherence to prescribed medications, attending regular monitoring appointments, understanding your condition and its warning signs, making lifestyle changes that support your health, and communicating openly with your healthcare team about any concerns or changes.

SECTION: Managing Type 2 Diabetes
Type 2 diabetes management aims to keep blood glucose levels within a target range to prevent short-term and long-term complications. Blood glucose targets are typically an HbA1c below 53 mmol/mol (7%) for most people, though individual targets are set based on age, complications, and other factors. Diet plays a central role: reduce refined carbohydrates and sugary foods, increase fibre, eat regular meals, and maintain a healthy weight. Physical activity improves insulin sensitivity. First-line medication is metformin, which reduces the liver's glucose output. Additional medications include SGLT2 inhibitors, GLP-1 agonists, DPP-4 inhibitors, and eventually insulin if other treatments are insufficient. People with type 2 diabetes should have annual checks including HbA1c, kidney function, cholesterol, blood pressure, eye screening (retinal photography), and foot assessment.

SECTION: Managing Hypertension (High Blood Pressure)
Hypertension management combines lifestyle modification and medication. Target blood pressure for most adults is below 140/90 mmHg, or below 130/80 mmHg for those with diabetes or kidney disease. Lifestyle measures include reducing salt intake to less than 6g per day, following the DASH diet (rich in fruits, vegetables, and low-fat dairy, low in saturated fat and sodium), exercising regularly, limiting alcohol, stopping smoking, and reducing stress. When lifestyle measures are insufficient, antihypertensive medications are prescribed. First-line medications depend on age and ethnicity: ACE inhibitors or angiotensin receptor blockers (ARBs) for adults under 55, calcium channel blockers for adults over 55 or Black African/Caribbean adults. Blood pressure should be checked at least annually and more frequently in those with known hypertension.

SECTION: Managing Asthma Long-Term
Long-term asthma management aims to achieve good symptom control, prevent exacerbations, and maintain normal lung function. Treatment follows a stepwise approach guided by symptom control. Step 1: short-acting beta-agonist (SABA) reliever inhaler (such as salbutamol) for as-needed use. Step 2: add a low-dose inhaled corticosteroid (ICS) preventer inhaler (such as beclometasone). Steps 3 and 4: add a long-acting beta-agonist (LABA) and increase ICS dose. Step 5: specialist referral and additional therapies. Asthma control is assessed by frequency of reliever inhaler use (more than 3 times per week suggests poor control), nighttime symptoms, and impact on daily activities. An annual asthma review with a GP or practice nurse is recommended. All patients should have a written personalised asthma action plan.

SECTION: Managing COPD (Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease)
COPD is a progressive lung condition predominantly caused by smoking that causes airflow limitation and breathing difficulty. It is characterised by chronic bronchitis, emphysema, or both. Symptoms include progressive breathlessness, chronic cough, and excess mucus production. Spirometry is required for diagnosis. COPD is staged by severity using the FEV1 measurement. Management includes smoking cessation (the single most important intervention), bronchodilator inhalers (short-acting for relief, long-acting for maintenance), inhaled corticosteroids in combination with long-acting bronchodilators for frequent exacerbators, pulmonary rehabilitation (exercise and education programme), influenza and pneumococcal vaccination, and in severe cases, long-term oxygen therapy. Exacerbations (sudden worsening) are managed with increased bronchodilators, antibiotics, and oral corticosteroids.

SECTION: Managing Heart Failure
Heart failure is a condition in which the heart cannot pump blood effectively enough to meet the body's demands. It can result from previous heart attack, hypertension, valvular disease, or cardiomyopathy. Symptoms include breathlessness (especially on exertion or when lying flat), ankle swelling, fatigue, and reduced exercise tolerance. Management includes ACE inhibitors or ARBs (which improve heart function and reduce mortality), beta-blockers (which reduce heart rate and improve cardiac function), diuretics (to reduce fluid retention and breathlessness), aldosterone antagonists, and SGLT2 inhibitors (which have shown significant benefit in heart failure with reduced ejection fraction). Daily weight monitoring allows early detection of fluid accumulation. Dietary salt restriction and fluid management are important. Patients should have regular specialist follow-up.

SECTION: Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD) Management
CKD is a long-term condition in which the kidneys do not work as well as they should. It is staged from G1 (mildly reduced function) to G5 (kidney failure requiring dialysis or transplantation). The most common causes are diabetes and hypertension. CKD is often asymptomatic in early stages and is detected through blood (eGFR, creatinine) and urine (protein) tests. Management aims to slow progression and manage complications. Key interventions include tight blood pressure control (target below 130/80 mmHg), ACE inhibitors or ARBs to protect the kidneys, blood glucose control in diabetes, avoiding nephrotoxic medications such as NSAIDs and certain contrast agents, managing anaemia, bone disease, and electrolyte imbalances as the condition progresses, and dietary modifications guided by a renal dietitian. Patients with stage 4 or 5 CKD should be referred to a nephrologist for specialist management and planning for renal replacement therapy if needed.

SECTION: Chronic Pain Management
Chronic pain is pain lasting more than 3 months and is distinct from acute pain. It may be associated with an underlying condition such as arthritis, fibromyalgia, or nerve damage, or may persist beyond the expected healing time of an injury. Chronic pain affects approximately 1 in 3 people in the UK and significantly impacts quality of life. Management is multimodal and typically involves a combination of pharmacological treatments (paracetamol, NSAIDs, low-dose antidepressants such as amitriptyline, anticonvulsants such as gabapentin, and in specific circumstances opioids), psychological therapies (CBT for pain, acceptance and commitment therapy), physiotherapy and exercise, pain management programmes, and complementary therapies such as acupuncture. Opioids are not recommended as first-line treatment for chronic non-cancer pain and carry risks of dependence and tolerance.

SECTION: Arthritis Management
There are over 100 types of arthritis. The two most common are osteoarthritis (OA) and rheumatoid arthritis (RA). Osteoarthritis management focuses on maintaining function and reducing pain through exercise (which is the most effective long-term treatment), weight management, physiotherapy, analgesics, and in advanced cases joint replacement surgery. Rheumatoid arthritis is an autoimmune condition causing systemic inflammation. Early aggressive treatment is essential to prevent joint damage. Disease-modifying antirheumatic drugs (DMARDs) including methotrexate are the cornerstone of treatment, alongside NSAIDs and corticosteroids for symptom management. Biological therapies (such as TNF inhibitors) are used when conventional DMARDs are insufficient. Regular monitoring of disease activity and treatment side effects is essential. Multidisciplinary care including rheumatology, physiotherapy, and occupational therapy optimises outcomes.

SECTION: Supporting Mental Wellbeing with Chronic Illness
Living with a chronic illness significantly increases the risk of developing depression and anxiety. The psychological burden of managing a long-term condition, coping with pain or disability, and uncertainty about the future can profoundly affect mental health and quality of life. It is important for healthcare teams to screen for depression and anxiety in patients with chronic conditions and offer appropriate support. Self-management strategies that support mental wellbeing include maintaining social connections, engaging in activities that provide meaning and enjoyment, setting realistic goals, using relaxation and mindfulness techniques, connecting with peer support groups and condition-specific charities, and seeking professional psychological support when needed. Addressing mental health is an integral part of comprehensive chronic disease management.

SECTION: Regular Monitoring and Follow-up in Chronic Disease
Regular monitoring is essential for people living with chronic conditions to track disease progression, assess treatment effectiveness, detect complications early, and adjust management plans. The frequency and type of monitoring depends on the condition and individual circumstances. For type 2 diabetes, annual checks include HbA1c, renal function, cholesterol, blood pressure, foot assessment, and eye screening. For hypertension, blood pressure should be checked at least annually and kidney function monitored if on ACE inhibitors or ARBs. For COPD, spirometry, review of inhaler technique, and exacerbation frequency should be assessed annually. It is important to attend all scheduled monitoring appointments even when feeling well, as many complications of chronic disease develop silently before causing symptoms.
